Section 01

Executive Summary

This report introduces and applies a novel diagnostic framework for assessing the health of liberal democracies. The framework is designed to be impartial, universally applicable, and capable of capturing the nuanced processes of both democratic resilience and decay. Its efficacy is validated against the historical case of democratic collapse in Germany from 1933 to 1938, ensuring its sensitivity to early warning signs of authoritarianism. The core of this report is the exhaustive application of this framework to the current administration of the United States, providing a data-driven, indicator-by-indicator analysis of the nation's democratic condition.

The diagnostic model is structured around three interdependent pillars: Pillar I: Foundational Institutions and the Rule of Law, which assesses the state's core architecture; Pillar II: Civil Liberties and the Public Sphere, which evaluates the space for public contestation and organization; and Pillar III: Political Culture and Norms, which measures the unwritten rules and shared values that animate the democratic system. These pillars are disaggregated into twelve specific, measurable indicators.

Key Finding: The assessment reveals a democratic system under significant and sustained stress. While the formal architecture of democracy—its constitutional rights, electoral processes, and legal structures—largely remains intact, the system is afflicted by a severe degradation of the political norms and institutional forbearance that are essential for its proper functioning.

Foundational institutions, particularly the judiciary and the electoral process, face acute politicization and a crisis of public trust. Civil liberties, while robustly protected by law, are challenged by a polluted information ecosystem, economic pressures on the free press, and targeted political attacks on academic freedom. Most critically, the political culture is characterized by a severe erosion of mutual toleration, the normalization of rhetoric that delegitimizes political opposition, and historically low levels of public confidence in the democratic process itself.

The overall diagnosis is not one of imminent collapse but of a "flawed democracy" exhibiting symptoms of chronic, low-grade democratic erosion, or "backsliding." The divergence in assessments from major global indices—with some rating the U.S. "Free" based on its formal rights and others "autocratizing" based on the decay of its processes—is itself a key indicator of this condition.

Section 02

Part I: A Diagnostic Framework for the Resilience and Decay of Liberal Democracy

1.1 Introduction to Democratic Assessment Methodologies

The measurement of democracy is a complex and contested field. Leading global indices, while invaluable, often produce divergent assessments of the same country due to differences in methodology, definitions, and the weighting of various democratic components.1 For instance, Freedom House's focus on foundational political rights and civil liberties may classify a country as "Free," while the Economist Intelligence Unit (EIU), with its emphasis on political culture, may deem it a "Flawed Democracy," and the Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) project, with its highly disaggregated data, might identify a process of "autocratization."2

This divergence is not a failure of measurement but a crucial finding in itself. For a stable, healthy democracy, assessments from credible indices should broadly converge. Significant divergence suggests a system in a state of contradiction or stress, where certain democratic elements remain strong while others are actively eroding. In the context of the United States, this points to a persistence of de jure constitutional rights coexisting with a severe degradation of de facto institutional processes and norms.

This new framework draws upon the structural clarity of Freedom House, the multidimensional and disaggregated approach of V-Dem, the EIU's critical focus on political culture, the citizen-centric perspective of International IDEA, and the concept of cultivating democratic values from the Council of Europe.

1.2 Historical Validation: The Point of No Return

To validate its relevance and sensitivity to severe democratic decay, each indicator in this framework is mapped to a key event or process during the collapse of the Weimar Republic and the consolidation of Nazi power in Germany between 1933 and 1938. This "stress test" ensures the framework can identify critical warning signs of authoritarianism.

Critical Insight: The erosion of Pillar III (Political Culture) and Pillar II (Civil Liberties)""such as the scapegoating of minorities, attacks on the press, and encouragement of political violence—are the early, often highly visible, warning signs. However, as long as Pillar I (Foundational Institutions) remains intact, a pathway to systemic self-correction theoretically exists through elections, judicial review, or legislative action. The true "exit signal" for a citizen assessing systemic risk is the collapse of Pillar I. When the legislature is neutered, the civil service purged, and the judiciary bypassed, the system loses its capacity for recourse and self-correction.

Section 03

Pillar A: Foundational Institutions and the Rule of Law

This pillar assesses the core architecture of the state: its electoral system, judiciary, and the checks and balances between branches of government. The health of these institutions is the ultimate guarantor of the rule of law and the primary bulwark against authoritarian consolidation.

1.1: Electoral Process Integrity & Pluralism
Score: 6.5/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Under Severe Stress

The formal machinery of U.S. elections remains functional, and the 2024 general election was conducted without evidence of widespread, outcome-altering fraud. However, the ecosystem surrounding the electoral process is deeply fractured and under severe partisan pressure. The V-Dem Institute has noted that the quality of elections is deteriorating in a significant number of countries, with the U.S. being a prominent case.4

The legislative landscape for voting rights is highly contested and increasingly divergent at the state level. In 2024 alone, at least 10 states enacted 19 laws that make it more difficult for eligible citizens to vote, while 21 states enacted 32 laws that expand voting access.40 This creates a patchwork of rules where the ease of casting a ballot depends heavily on a voter's geographic location.

1.2: Judicial Independence & Adherence to Rulings
Score: 6.0/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Declining

The U.S. judiciary remains structurally independent and its rulings are, for the most part, obeyed. However, it is experiencing a severe crisis of legitimacy driven by intense and escalating politicization. A 2024 study of over 400,000 federal appellate cases found that partisan alignment significantly influences outcomes, even in non-ideological cases.47

The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in Trump v. U.S., which granted former presidents absolute immunity from criminal prosecution for "core constitutional" official acts, was seen by its dissenters as placing the presidency "above the law."17 Public confidence in the judicial system has plummeted to a record low of 35%, a 24-point drop since 2020.48

1.3: Legislative & Executive Checks and Balances
Score: 6.0/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Weakening

The formal system of checks and balances between the legislative and executive branches remains in place, but its effectiveness has been significantly weakened by the erosion of unwritten norms of forbearance and mutual respect. The legislature has become so crippled by gridlock that even basic functions, such as passing appropriations bills, are routinely held hostage.39 Politicians are increasingly willing to exploit the letter of constitutional rules in ways that violate their spirit.21

1.4: Non-Politicization of the Civil Service & Security
Score: 6.5/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: At Risk

The United States has a long-standing tradition of a professional, non-partisan civil service. This norm is now under direct and sustained attack. A key tactic in the modern authoritarian playbook is to politicize independent institutions by purging officials deemed insufficiently loyal.22 There are ongoing plans and proposals to dismantle civil service protections and enable mass firings of federal employees to be replaced with loyalists.

Section 04

Pillar B: Civil Liberties and the Public Sphere

This pillar examines the freedoms that allow citizens to debate, organize, and contest power. It includes the freedom of the press, the right to form independent organizations, and freedom from undue state interference.

2.1: Freedom of Expression, Press, and Information
Score: 7.5/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Contradictory

The United States continues to have strong legal protections for freedom of expression enshrined in the First Amendment.26 However, the public sphere is afflicted by a severely degraded information ecosystem. The digital sphere is saturated with false, misleading, and conspiratorial content—a form of "censorship by noise" that undermines informed debate.22 In 2024, at least 49 journalists were arrested and 80 were assaulted while doing their jobs.51

2.2: Associational and Organizational Rights
Score: 8.5/10 — Stable

Assessment: Robust but Under Pressure

American civil society is vibrant, diverse, and highly active. A dense network of NGOs, advocacy groups, and grassroots movements plays a critical role in policy debates and public mobilization.25 Organizations like the ACLU and the Brennan Center are at the forefront of legal challenges to actions undermining democratic norms.53 However, this pillar is operating in an increasingly hostile political environment.

2.3: Academic and Cultural Freedom
Score: 6.5/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Declining

Academic freedom in the United States is facing a coordinated and politically motivated assault, primarily at the state level. Since 2021, a wave of educational "gag orders" has swept through state legislatures. PEN America estimates that these restrictions have directly affected 1.3 million public school teachers and 100,000 higher education faculty.30

2.4: Protection from Illegitimate State Force
Score: 6.0/10 — Stable

Assessment: Mixed with Significant Concerns

While the U.S. does not engage in widespread, systematic repression, there are chronic issues regarding surveillance and the criminal justice system. Congress reauthorized Section 702 of FISA without significant reforms.26 The incarceration rate remains among the highest in the world, with stark racial disparities.2

Section 05

Pillar C: Political Culture and Norms

This pillar assesses the informal "rules of the game—"the shared values, beliefs, and behaviors that underpin a democracy's formal institutions. The erosion of these norms is often the earliest and most telling indicator of democratic backsliding.

3.1: Acceptance of Legitimate Political Opposition
Score: 4.5/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Severely Degraded

The norm of mutual toleration—the idea that political opponents are legitimate adversaries with a right to govern, not treasonous enemies—has been severely degraded. This is arguably the most critical symptom of democratic decay currently present in the country. Leaders increasingly employ language that casts opponents as corrupt, un-American, and an existential threat.18

3.2: Rejection of Political Violence
Score: 5.5/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Declining

The most stark example was the January 6, 2021, attack on the U.S. Capitol—an act of political violence intended to disrupt the peaceful transfer of power.2 Beyond this, there has been a proliferation of threats and intimidation targeting elected officials, judges, and election administrators. The percentage of Americans who believe violence is "sometimes justified" has doubled since 2017.14

3.3: Equal Protection & Minority Rights
Score: 6.0/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Under Pressure

The racial wealth gap has remained largely unchanged for 50 years, with Black families holding just 24 cents for every dollar of white family wealth.60 Structural inequalities are compounded by restrictive voting laws with disproportionate impact on minority communities, abusive border policies, and new threats to LGBTQ rights.2

3.4: Public Trust in Democratic Processes
Score: 4.0/10 ▼ Declining

Assessment: Critically Low and Declining

Public trust in democratic institutions is at a historic nadir. A 2024 Pew poll found that 72% of Americans believe the U.S. used to be a good example of democracy but no longer is.38 A record 80% believe Americans are greatly divided on the most important values.63 Trust in the federal government remains near historic lows, with only 18% saying they trust it to do what is right most of the time.39

Section 06

Part III: Systemic Pressures and Institutional Resilience

The symptoms of democratic ill-health are driven by deep, systemic pressures that have been building for decades.

3.1 The Role of Money and Influence

The influence of money in American politics fundamentally undermines the democratic principle of political equality. The 2024 election cycle broke all previous records: presidential candidates raised over $2 billion, congressional candidates raised $3.8 billion, and PACs raised an astronomical $15.7 billion.66 The Brennan Center reports that the 2024 election saw more money than ever from wealthy megadonors, dwarfing the contributions of small donors.67

3.2 The Digital Sphere: Mobilization vs. Fragmentation

Digital technology is a profoundly double-edged sword. A majority of Americans believe social media has been bad for democracy: 74% of Republicans and 57% of Democrats.62 The advent of generative AI adds a dangerous new dimension, with campaigns using AI-generated images to attack opponents.68

3.3 Socio-Economic Divides

Decades of rising economic inequality, wage stagnation, and eroding social mobility have created fertile ground for political discontent and polarization. The failure of democratic institutions to deliver adequate socioeconomic goods can lead voters to abandon them for populist, anti-establishment leaders.65

Section 07

Part IV: Pathways and Prognosis

4.1 Identifying Critical Vulnerabilities

The most acute vulnerability is the dangerous feedback loop created by the convergence of three trends: the collapse of public trust (3.4), the rejection of the legitimacy of political opposition (3.1), and the escalating politicization of foundational institutions (Pillar I).

When citizens lose faith in the system, they become receptive to leaders who amplify this cynicism. These leaders then employ rhetoric casting opponents as enemies, creating a political permission structure to attack constraining institutions. Each successful attack further validates public cynicism, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of decay. This is the core dynamic of democratic backsliding observed globally.

4.2 Sources of Resilience

Despite these severe stresses, the U.S. retains significant sources of resilience:

  • The Broader Judiciary: Lower courts have repeatedly ruled against executive overreach, demonstrating commitment to the rule of law over partisan loyalty.2
  • Civil Society: A diverse ecosystem of legal organizations, advocacy groups, and grassroots movements actively counters democratic erosion.55
  • Federalism: State governments can serve as laboratories of democracy and refuges for democratic practice.40
  • Political Engagement: Voter turnout has reached historic highs, suggesting continued citizen investment.62

4.3 Strategic Recommendations

  1. Depoliticize Foundational Institutions: Federal legislation for national election standards; a binding Supreme Court ethics code; strengthened civil service protections.
  2. Combat Disinformation: Robust transparency laws for online political advertising; public support for independent journalism; media literacy education.
  3. Bridge Partisan Divides: Investment in civic organizations facilitating inter-group dialogue and collaboration.63
  4. Address Economic Discontent: Policies that reduce wealth disparities, strengthen the social safety net, and provide greater economic security.63
